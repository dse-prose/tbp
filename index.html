The Third Book of Pietro Bembo's Prose

XML/TEI encoding for a digital scholarly edition

The project

The main focus of the project is to produce an XML/TEI file compliant with the TEI P5 Guidelines and to propose a digital edition mockup tailored on the case study of Bembo's Prose. The first seven chapters of Book Third have been selected as a representative sample of Bembo's work.
Together with elements required for the representation of primary sources, the markup model addresses the encoding of textual variation across multiple witnesses, as well as other content information such as Named Entities, sample words mentioned by Bembo in the exemplification of grammar rules and quotations.
Based on the resulting XML/TEI file, the website also presents a proposal for a digital scholarly edition of Bembo's dialogue.

Introduction to Bembo's Prose

Pietro Bembo’s Prose – commonly known with the title Prose della volgar lingua – is a treatise which had a deep influence on both Italian language and literature. Bembo, through the conversation between the characters of his work, sets the ground for the normalization of vernacular language. In particular, the three parts in which the book is divided cover the following topics:

  • First Book: origins of vernacular
  • Second Book: rhetoric and stylistic considerations
  • Third Book: grammar rules

The dialogue is set in Venice, where Carlo Bembo hosts a dinner during which he and his guests, Ercole Strozzi, Federigo Fregoso and Giuliano de’ Medici, engage in a conversation about vernacular language. The three books by which the work is composed correspond to the three evenings, from 10 to 12 December 1502, over which the conversation spans.

The dialogue was first published in 1525 by the editor Giovanni Tacuino in Venice and dedicated to pope Clemens VII. A second edition was published, again in Venice, by Francesco Marcolini in 1538, while the last printed edition – published in 1549, after the death of the author – was printed by Lorenzo Torrentino in Florence.
The elaboration that lead towards the text attested by the princeps is documented by a fourth witness, manuscript Vat.lat. 3210.

Although not selected as a witness for the project, a fifth exemplar should be mentioned for the sake of completeness. In 2018, a group of scholars found the annotated copy of the princeps (Bertolo-Cursi-Pulsoni 2018) on which Bembo wrote corrections and insertions that were incorporated first in the second edition and later in the third. The edition of this annotated princeps is significant not only because it sheds light on how Bembo revised his text, but also because some of the marginalia contain readings not attested in the printed tradition and can therefore be regarded as the last will of the author.
